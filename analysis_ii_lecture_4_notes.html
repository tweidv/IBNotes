Uniform Convergence and Series of Functions

▶0:09

Review of Previous Theorem

We proved a theorem last time which stated that for a sequence of functions $(f_n)$ on some interval, if:

  • The sequence of derivatives $(f_n')$ converges uniformly to some function $g$
  • The original sequence $(f_n)$ converges at one point

Then the sequence $(f_n)$ converges uniformly to some function $f$, and moreover $f$ is differentiable with $f' = g$.

▶2:43

Derivatives Need Not Be Continuous

Note that $f'$ need not be continuous. Just because a function is differentiable doesn't mean that the derivative needs to be continuous.

▶3:18

Function with Discontinuous Derivative

Consider the function defined by:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 \sin(1/x) & \text{if } x \neq 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } x = 0 \end{cases}$$

on the interval $[-1,1]$. You can check that this function is differentiable everywhere, but the derivative is not continuous at $x = 0$. The derivative is given by:

$$f'(x) = \begin{cases} 2x\sin(1/x) - \cos(1/x) & \text{if } x \neq 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } x = 0 \end{cases}$$

This derivative is not continuous at $x = 0$.

▶6:31

Intermediate Value Property for Derivatives

However, it's interesting to note that if $f$ is differentiable on some interval, then $f'$ satisfies the intermediate value property. This means that for any $c$ between $f'(a)$ and $f'(b)$, there exists a point $y$ in the interval $(a,b)$ such that $f'(y) = c$.

This is a nice fact - even though the derivative might not be continuous, it still satisfies this familiar property of continuous functions. This provides some measure of continuity for derivatives.

▶9:12

Advanced Examples (Non-examinable)

There are examples where derivatives can have large sets of discontinuities. For instance, the Volterra function gives you a big set of points of discontinuity for the derivative. However, this is beyond the scope of the course.

▶10:47

Alternative Proof Using Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

If we assume that $f_n'$ is continuous (in addition to the other hypotheses), then we can give a much more straightforward proof based on the fundamental theorem of calculus rather than the mean value theorem.

Since $f_n'$ is continuous, we have by the fundamental theorem of calculus:

$$f_n(x) = f_n(c) + \int_c^x f_n'(t) \, dt$$

Now, to show uniform convergence, we consider:

$$|f_n(x) - f_m(x)| \leq |f_n(c) - f_m(c)| + \left|\int_c^x (f_n'(t) - f_m'(t)) \, dt\right|$$

Using the triangle inequality and uniform convergence of derivatives:

$$\leq |f_n(c) - f_m(c)| + \sup_{t \in [a,b]} |f_n'(t) - f_m'(t)| \cdot |x - c|$$

Both terms go to zero as $n, m \to \infty$, so $f_n$ is uniformly Cauchy and hence converges uniformly.

To prove that $f$ is differentiable with $f' = g$, we take the limit in the fundamental theorem identity:

$$f(x) = f(c) + \int_c^x g(t) \, dt$$

Since $g$ is continuous (being the uniform limit of continuous functions), we can apply the fundamental theorem again to get $f'(x) = g(x)$.

▶20:32

Algebraic Properties of Uniform Convergence

Suppose we have two sequences of functions $(f_n)$ and $(g_n)$ on some set $E$, and:

  • $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $E$
  • $g_n \to g$ uniformly on $E$

Then:

(a) $f_n + g_n \to f + g$ uniformly on $E$

(b) If $h$ is a bounded function on $E$, then $h \cdot f_n \to h \cdot f$ uniformly on $E$

Proof omitted - this is typically covered in example sheets.

▶22:39

Why Boundedness is Required

Note that boundedness of $h$ is essential in part (b). A simple example to show this is false without boundedness:

Take $f_n(x) = \frac{1}{n}$ (constant function) and $h(x) = x$ on $\mathbb{R}$. Then $f_n \to 0$ uniformly, but $h \cdot f_n = \frac{x}{n}$ does not converge uniformly to $0$ on $\mathbb{R}$ because $\sup_{x \in \mathbb{R}} \left|\frac{x}{n}\right| = \infty$ for every $n$.

Even on a bounded interval like $[0,1]$, if we take $h(x) = \frac{1}{x}$ on $(0,1]$, then $h$ is unbounded and the product $h \cdot f_n = \frac{1}{nx}$ doesn't converge uniformly.

▶26:46

Series of Functions

We now extend the notion of series from real numbers to functions. Given a sequence of functions $(g_n)$ defined on some set $E$, we define the partial sums:

$$f_n(x) = \sum_{j=1}^n g_j(x)$$

The series $\sum_{j=1}^\infty g_j(x)$ converges pointwise at a point $x \in E$ if the sequence of partial sums $(f_n(x))$ converges.

The series converges uniformly on $E$ if the sequence of partial sums $(f_n)$ converges uniformly on $E$.

▶31:17

Absolute Convergence for Series of Functions

We can also define absolute convergence for series of functions:

  • Pointwise absolute convergence: The series $\sum_{j=1}^\infty g_j(x)$ converges absolutely at a point $x$ if $\sum_{j=1}^\infty |g_j(x)|$ converges.
  • Absolute uniform convergence: The series converges absolutely and uniformly on $E$ if both $\sum_{j=1}^\infty g_j$ and $\sum_{j=1}^\infty |g_j|$ converge uniformly on $E$.
▶36:14

Absolute Uniform Convergence Implies Uniform Convergence

If the series $\sum_{j=1}^\infty g_j$ converges absolutely and uniformly on $E$, then it converges uniformly on $E$.

Proof: We need to show that the partial sums are uniformly Cauchy. For $n > m$:

$$\sup_{x \in E} \left|\sum_{j=m+1}^n g_j(x)\right| \leq \sup_{x \in E} \sum_{j=m+1}^n |g_j(x)|$$

Since $\sum |g_j|$ converges uniformly, the right-hand side tends to zero as $n, m \to \infty$. Therefore, the partial sums are uniformly Cauchy and hence converge uniformly.

▶42:27

Counterexample: Uniform + Pointwise Absolute ≠ Absolute Uniform

The converse is false. Consider the series $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n} x^n$ on the interval $(0,1)$.

This series:

  • Converges absolutely at each point $x$ (since $|x| < 1$, so $|x|^n/n$ is dominated by a convergent geometric series)
  • Converges uniformly on $(0,1)$

However, it does not converge absolutely uniformly. The series of absolute values is $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n}$.

To see why this doesn't converge uniformly, consider the difference between partial sums:

$$\left|\sum_{j=m+1}^n \frac{x^j}{j}\right| \geq \sum_{j=m+1}^n \frac{x^n}{j}$$

Taking the supremum over $x \in (0,1)$, we get $\sup_{x \in (0,1)} \left|\sum_{j=m+1}^n \frac{x^j}{j}\right| \geq \sum_{j=m+1}^n \frac{1}{j}$, which tends to infinity as $n \to \infty$ for any fixed $m$.
