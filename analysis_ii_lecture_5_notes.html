Review of Convergence Types

From the previous lecture, we defined several notions of convergence for series of functions:

  • Convergence at a point: The series converges at a specific point
  • Uniform convergence: The series converges uniformly on some domain
  • Absolute convergence at a point: The series of absolute values converges at a point
  • Absolute uniform convergence: The strongest notion - both absolute and uniform convergence

Weierstrass M-Test

Let $(g_n)$ be a sequence of functions defined on a subset $E$ of $\mathbb{R}$, and suppose that for every $n$, there exists a number $M_n$ such that:

$$\sup_{x \in E} |g_n(x)| \leq M_n$$

Suppose further that the series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} M_n$ converges. Then the series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} g_n(x)$ converges absolutely uniformly on $E$.

This test provides a practical criterion for determining absolute uniform convergence.

▶06:06

Proof of Weierstrass M-Test

We want to show that the series of absolute value functions converges uniformly. Let $S_n(x) = \sum_{j=1}^n |g_j(x)|$ be the partial sums.

For $n > m$, consider the difference:

$$|S_n(x) - S_m(x)| = \left|\sum_{j=m+1}^n |g_j(x)|\right| = \sum_{j=m+1}^n |g_j(x)|$$

By the hypothesis, $|g_j(x)| \leq M_j$ for all $x \in E$, so:

$$\sum_{j=m+1}^n |g_j(x)| \leq \sum_{j=m+1}^n M_j$$

Since $\sum M_j$ converges, $\sum_{j=m+1}^n M_j \to 0$ as $m, n \to \infty$. Therefore:

$$\sup_{x \in E} |S_n(x) - S_m(x)| \leq \sum_{j=m+1}^n M_j \to 0$$

This shows that $(S_n)$ is uniformly Cauchy, hence uniformly convergent.

Power Series

A power series centered at $a$ is a series of the form:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n(x-a)^n$$

where $c_n$ and $a$ are real numbers. The terms are:

  • $n=0$: $c_0$ (constant term)
  • $n=1$: $c_1(x-a)$
  • $n=2$: $c_2(x-a)^2$
  • And so on...

The coefficients $c_n$ are fixed constants independent of $x$.

Radius of Convergence Theorem

For any power series $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n(x-a)^n$, there exists a number $R$ (which may be $0$ or $\infty$) called the radius of convergence, such that:

Part A: If $|x-a| < R$, then the power series converges absolutely at $x$.

Part B: If $|x-a| > R$, then the power series diverges.

The radius of convergence is given by:

$$R = \frac{1}{\limsup_{n \to \infty} |c_n|^{1/n}}$$

where we interpret $1/0 = \infty$ and $1/\infty = 0$.

▶21:29

Uniform Convergence in Interior

For any $r$ with $0 < r < R$, the power series converges uniformly on the closed interval $[a-r, a+r]$.

Moreover, the function $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n(x-a)^n$ is continuous on the entire open interval $(a-R, a+R)$.

Note: At every point in the interval of convergence, we get pointwise convergence, but for uniform convergence, we need to stay strictly in the interior.

▶26:10

Proof of Uniform Convergence

For part B of the theorem, we use the Weierstrass M-test. Consider a point $x = a + r$ where $0 < r < R$.

Since $x = a + r$ is in the interval of convergence, the series $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |c_n|r^n$ converges absolutely.

For any $x$ in $[a-r, a+r]$, we have $|x - a| \leq r$, so:

$$|c_n(x-a)^n| = |c_n| \cdot |x-a|^n \leq |c_n| \cdot r^n = M_n$$

Since $\sum M_n = \sum |c_n|r^n$ converges, the Weierstrass M-test applies, showing that the power series converges absolutely uniformly on $[a-r, a+r]$.

Since each partial sum is a polynomial (hence continuous), and we have uniform convergence, the limit function $f$ is continuous on this interval.

Boundary Behavior of Power Series

This is largely an exercise topic, but important to understand.

If a power series with radius of convergence $R$ (finite and positive) converges at one of the boundary points, say at $x = a + R$, then:

$$\lim_{x \to (a+R)^-} f(x) = f(a+R)$$

This means the function can be extended continuously to include that boundary point.

Moreover, under this condition, the series converges uniformly on intervals of the form $[a-r, a+R]$ for any $0 < r < R$.

This is relevant for solving boundary value problems in partial differential equations, where we need solutions continuous up to the boundary.

Differentiation of Power Series

Let $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n(x-a)^n$ be a power series with radius of convergence $R > 0$.

Part 1: The derived series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n(x-a)^{n-1}$ has the same radius of convergence $R$.

Part 2: The function $f$ is differentiable on $(a-R, a+R)$, and:

$$f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n(x-a)^{n-1}$$

This means we can differentiate power series term by term, and the result has the same radius of convergence.

▶43:17

Power Series are Infinitely Differentiable

Functions defined by power series are infinitely often differentiable on their interval of convergence. We can repeatedly apply the differentiation theorem:

$$f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n(x-a)^{n-1}$$

$$f''(x) = \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} n(n-1) c_n(x-a)^{n-2}$$

$$f^{(k)}(x) = \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} c_n(x-a)^{n-k}$$

Each derived series has the same radius of convergence as the original.

▶47:40

Proof of Differentiation Theorem

Part 1: To show the derived series has the same radius of convergence, we use the formula:

$$\limsup_{n \to \infty} |n c_n|^{1/(n-1)} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} |c_n|^{1/n} \cdot \lim_{n \to \infty} n^{1/(n-1)}$$

Since $\lim_{n \to \infty} n^{1/n} = 1$, the radius of convergence is unchanged.

Part 2: We apply the theorem from Lecture 3 about interchanging limits and derivatives. The partial sums $f_n(x) = \sum_{j=0}^n c_j(x-a)^j$ are polynomials, hence differentiable everywhere.

The derivatives $f_n'(x) = \sum_{j=1}^n j c_j(x-a)^{j-1}$ converge uniformly on any closed interval $[a-r, a+r]$ with $0 < r < R$ (by Part 1 and the radius of convergence theorem).

Since $f_n(a) = c_0$ converges, we have the anchor point condition. Therefore, by the interchange theorem:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n(x-a)^{n-1}$$
