Review of Finite Dimensional Linear Algebra

Recall from 1A Vectors and Matrices: A linear map $A$ from an $n$-dimensional vector space to itself is called Hermitian if $A^\dagger = A$, or equivalently, if $\mathbf{x} \cdot A\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}$ for all $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in V_n$.

▶02:16

Properties of Hermitian Operators

Hermitian operators have several nice properties:

  1. Eigenvalues are real
  2. Eigenvectors with distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal
  3. Spectral theorem: You can pick an orthogonal set of eigenvectors $\{v_i\}$ such that for each $\mathbf{x} \in V_n$, we can write $\mathbf{x} = \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{x}_i v_i$ where $\hat{x}_i = \frac{\mathbf{x} \cdot v_i}{\|v_i\|^2}$
▶04:49

Complex Inner Product Space

We assume a complex inner product space where the inner product is defined as $\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \overline{y_i}$ (complex conjugated). All discussions of orthogonality refer to this complex inner product.

▶05:11

Infinite Dimensional Extension

Today's question: What if $n$ is infinite? How much of this finite-dimensional theory carries over to infinite-dimensional vector spaces? The infinite is not just "finite but very big" - it's fundamentally different, and unfortunately, some properties from finite-dimensional linear algebra don't carry over.

▶05:45

Function Space

We introduce a vector space of "nice" functions $f$ from some closed interval $[a,b]$ to $\mathbb{C}$. The term "nice" will be elaborated on later, but for now, it means functions with sufficient regularity properties.

▶06:30

Weighted Inner Product

We introduce an inner product on our function space, denoted with subscript $w$:

$$\langle f, g \rangle_w = \int_a^b f(x) \overline{g(x)} w(x) \, dx$$

where $w$ is real-valued and positive on the open interval $(a,b)$. We call $w$ the weight function associated with the inner product.

▶08:02

Associated Norm

With the inner product comes an associated norm:

$$\|f\|_w = \sqrt{\langle f, f \rangle_w}$$

When the weight function is identically 1, we simply write $\langle f, g \rangle$ and $\|f\|$ without the subscript.

▶09:04

Self-Adjoint Differential Operator

A linear differential operator $L$ is said to be self-adjoint on $V$ with its inner product if:

$$\langle L y_1, y_2 \rangle_w = \langle y_1, L y_2 \rangle_w$$

for all $y_1, y_2 \in V$.

▶10:33

Eigenfunction-Eigenvalue Pair

We say that $(y, \lambda)$ where $y \in V$ (non-zero) and $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ is an eigenfunction-eigenvalue pair for $L$ if:

$$L y = \lambda y$$

▶12:00

Properties of Self-Adjoint Operators

If $L$ is self-adjoint on $V$ with the inner product, then:

  1. Eigenvalues are all real
  2. Eigenfunctions with distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal (with respect to the inner product)
  3. There exists a complete orthogonal set of eigenfunctions $\{y_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ such that for each $f \in V$, we can write $f = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \hat{f}_n y_n$ where $\hat{f}_n = \frac{\langle f, y_n \rangle_w}{\|y_n\|^2}$
▶14:58

Infinite Sum Convergence

The infinite sum in the spectral theorem implicitly means the limit of partial sums. The convergence here should be understood as convergence in norm (though for practical purposes in this course, it will likely work out pointwise as well).

▶15:38

Proof of Real Eigenvalues

Proof of (1): If $L y = \lambda y$ with $y \neq 0$, then:

$$(\lambda - \overline{\lambda}) \langle y, y \rangle_w = \langle L y, y \rangle_w - \langle y, L y \rangle_w = 0$$

Since $L$ is self-adjoint, the right-hand side is zero. Since $\langle y, y \rangle_w \neq 0$, we have $\lambda = \overline{\lambda}$, so $\lambda$ is real.

▶17:00

Proof of Orthogonality

Proof of (2): If $L y_1 = \lambda_1 y_1$ and $L y_2 = \lambda_2 y_2$ with $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$, then:

$$(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2) \langle y_1, y_2 \rangle_w = \langle L y_1, y_2 \rangle_w - \langle y_1, L y_2 \rangle_w = 0$$

Since $L$ is self-adjoint and $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$, we have $\langle y_1, y_2 \rangle_w = 0$, so the eigenfunctions are orthogonal.

▶18:49

Proof by Authority

Proof of (3): This is a proof by authority - David Hilbert said it was true, which implies it was true. This is not admissible in Tripos exams, but it's completely acceptable in Methods. The rigorous proof requires the spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators on Hilbert space (covered in Part II Linear Analysis).

▶20:15

Sturm-Liouville Operators and Boundary Values

We will study eigenvalue problems of the form $L y = \lambda y$ where $L$ is a differential operator, solved inside some interval $x \in [a,b]$ with boundary conditions at $x = a$ and $x = b$. Here $L$ will be self-adjoint and of a particular type: a Sturm-Liouville operator.

▶22:27

Sturm-Liouville Operator

A differential operator $L$ is a Sturm-Liouville operator on $[a,b]$ if it has the form:

$$L = \frac{1}{w(x)} \left[ -\frac{d}{dx}\left(p(x)\frac{d}{dx}\right) + q(x) \right]$$

where $p$, $q$, and $w$ are real-valued, and $p$ and $w$ are both positive on the open interval $(a,b)$. The function $w$ is called the weight function.

▶25:26

Eigenvalue Problem Form

If we multiply $L y = \lambda y$ by $w(x)$, we get:

$$-\frac{d}{dx}\left(p(x)\frac{dy}{dx}\right) + q(x) y = \lambda w(x) y$$

This looks like a differential equation, but we don't know what $\lambda$ is - we're trying to find both the eigenfunctions $y$ and eigenvalues $\lambda$ that satisfy this equation.

▶28:35

Singular Endpoint

For a Sturm-Liouville operator on $[a,b]$, an endpoint $c$ (either $a$ or $b$) is singular if $p(c) = 0$, and non-singular otherwise.

We will only impose boundary conditions at non-singular endpoints.

▶29:56

Real Homogeneous Boundary Conditions

At each non-singular endpoint $c$, we impose boundary conditions of the form:

$$\alpha_c y(c) + \beta_c y'(c) = 0$$

where $\alpha_c$ and $\beta_c$ are real numbers (not both zero).

▶32:26

Function Space V

We work on the vector space $V$ consisting of functions $y$ that are:

  • Twice continuously differentiable on the closed interval $[a,b]$
  • Bounded on the closed interval with bounded derivatives
  • Such that $y$ satisfies real homogeneous boundary conditions at each non-singular endpoint

We equip $V$ with the inner product $\langle f, g \rangle_w = \int_a^b f(x) \overline{g(x)} w(x) \, dx$.

▶34:55

Example 1: Cosine Weight Function

Consider the problem:

$$-\frac{d}{dx}\left(\cos\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)\frac{dy}{dx}\right) + \sin\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) y = \lambda x y$$

on the interval $[0, \pi]$ with boundary condition $y(0) = 0$.

Here: $p(x) = \cos(x/2)$, $q(x) = \sin(x/2)$, $w(x) = x$.

At $x = \pi$: $p(\pi) = \cos(\pi/2) = 0$, so $x = \pi$ is a singular endpoint. We only need boundary conditions at the non-singular endpoint $x = 0$.

▶37:48

Example 2: Legendre Polynomials

Consider the problem:

$$-\frac{d}{dx}\left((1-x^2)\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = \lambda y$$

on the interval $[-1, 1]$.

Here: $p(x) = 1-x^2$, $q(x) = 0$, $w(x) = 1$.

At $x = \pm 1$: $p(\pm 1) = 0$, so both endpoints are singular. No boundary conditions are needed!

The associated inner product is $\langle f, g \rangle = \int_{-1}^1 f(x) \overline{g(x)} \, dx$.

▶39:34

Self-Adjointness of Sturm-Liouville Operators

If $L$ is a Sturm-Liouville operator on $[a,b]$ with weight function $w$, and $y_1, y_2$ are twice continuously differentiable functions on the interval, then:

$$\langle L y_1, y_2 \rangle_w - \langle y_1, L y_2 \rangle_w = [p(x) W(y_1, y_2)]_{x=a}^{x=b}$$

where $W(y_1, y_2) = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1'$ is the Wronskian.

In particular, if $y_1, y_2 \in V$ (the function space with appropriate boundary conditions), then $L$ is self-adjoint on $V$ with the inner product specified by the weight function.

▶42:13

Proof of Self-Adjointness

The proof involves expanding the left-hand side using the definition of $L$ and integrating by parts. After cancellation of terms involving $q(x)$, we obtain:

$$\int_a^b \left[ y_1 \frac{d}{dx}(p y_2') - y_2 \frac{d}{dx}(p y_1') \right] dx = [p(x) W(y_1, y_2)]_{x=a}^{x=b}$$

This follows from the fundamental theorem of calculus and the fact that $\frac{d}{dx}[p(x) W(y_1, y_2)] = y_1 \frac{d}{dx}(p y_2') - y_2 \frac{d}{dx}(p y_1')$.

▶46:21

Boundary Term Analysis

For the boundary terms to vanish (making $L$ self-adjoint):

  • At singular endpoints: Since $p(c) = 0$, the boundary term automatically vanishes.
  • At non-singular endpoints: Since both $y_1$ and $y_2$ satisfy the same real homogeneous boundary condition $\alpha_c y(c) + \beta_c y'(c) = 0$, and $\alpha_c, \beta_c$ are real, we can show that the Wronskian $W(y_1, y_2)$ evaluated at $c$ is zero.

This is because the boundary conditions form a linear system with a non-zero determinant, forcing the Wronskian to be zero.

▶51:43

Historical Context

The history of Sturm-Liouville operators is fascinating - this is where analysis really started to get going. Before Cauchy and Weierstrass, it was "a jungle out there" with no rigorous foundations. The development of this theory shows how functional analysis was effectively born from these concrete problems.
