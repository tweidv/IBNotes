Uniform Convergence and Integration

Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of integrable functions on a bounded interval $[a,b]$ that converges uniformly to $f$. Then $f$ is integrable and

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_a^b f_n(x) \, dx = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx$$

Remark: The assumption that $f$ is integrable is actually redundant - uniform convergence of integrable functions automatically implies that the limit is integrable.

▶5:13

Proof of Integration Theorem

We need to show that $\left| \int_a^b f_n(x) \, dx - \int_a^b f(x) \, dx \right| \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.

By properties of integrals:

$$\left| \int_a^b f_n(x) \, dx - \int_a^b f(x) \, dx \right| = \left| \int_a^b (f_n(x) - f(x)) \, dx \right| \leq \int_a^b |f_n(x) - f(x)| \, dx$$

Since $f_n \to f$ uniformly, we have $\sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0$. Therefore:

$$\int_a^b |f_n(x) - f(x)| \, dx \leq \sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \cdot (b-a) \to 0$$

This completes the proof.

Differentiation Question

Now we turn to the question: if $(f_n)$ is a sequence of differentiable functions that converges uniformly to $f$, what can we say about differentiability of $f$ and convergence of the derivatives?

Ideally, we would like to say that:

  • $f$ is differentiable
  • $f_n'(x) \to f'(x)$ for each $x$
However, uniform convergence alone is not sufficient to guarantee either of these conclusions.

▶8:27

Uniform Convergence Alone is Insufficient

If $f_n \to f$ uniformly on some interval and each $f_n$ is differentiable, it does not follow that:

(a) $f$ is differentiable, or

(b) even if $f$ is differentiable, that $f_n'(x) \to f'(x)$ pointwise.

We need additional assumptions beyond uniform convergence.

▶10:19

Example 1: Non-differentiable Limit

Consider the sequence $f_n(x) = |x|^{1 + 1/n}$ on the interval $[-1,1]$.

Properties of $f_n$:

  • Each $f_n$ is differentiable everywhere (including at $x = 0$)
  • At $x = 0$: $f_n'(0) = 0$ (using difference quotient)
  • For $x \neq 0$: $f_n'(x) = \pm (1 + 1/n)|x|^{1/n}$ (sign depends on $x$)

Limit behavior:

$f_n(x) \to |x|$ uniformly on $[-1,1]$, but $|x|$ is not differentiable at $x = 0$.

This shows that uniform convergence of differentiable functions does not guarantee a differentiable limit.

▶14:34

Example 2: Derivatives Don't Converge

Consider $f_n(x) = \frac{\sin(nx)}{\sqrt{n}}$ on $\mathbb{R}$.

Function convergence:

$f_n(x) \to 0$ uniformly because $|f_n(x)| \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \to 0$.

Derivative behavior:

$f_n'(x) = \sqrt{n} \cos(nx)$, which does not converge (e.g., at $x = 0$, $f_n'(0) = \sqrt{n} \to \infty$).

This shows that even when the limit function is differentiable (constant function), the derivatives need not converge.

Main Theorem: Interchanging Limits and Derivatives

Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of differentiable functions on a closed interval $[a,b]$ with the following two assumptions:

Assumption 1: $f_n' \to g$ uniformly on $[a,b]$ for some function $g$

Assumption 2: There exists $c \in [a,b]$ such that $(f_n(c))$ converges

Then:

(a) $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $[a,b]$ for some function $f$

(b) $f$ is differentiable on $[a,b]$ and $f' = g$

Key insight: This allows us to interchange differentiation and taking limits: $\frac{d}{dx}\left(\lim_{n \to \infty} f_n(x)\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{d}{dx}f_n(x)$

▶20:21

Why These Assumptions?

Assumption 1 (uniform convergence of derivatives): This gives us control over the derivatives, allowing us to use the Mean Value Theorem to connect function values to derivative values.

Assumption 2 (convergence at one point): This prevents the sequence from "escaping to infinity" by anchoring it at one point. Without this, we could have $f_n(x) = n$ with $f_n'(x) = 0$ - the derivatives converge uniformly to 0, but the functions don't converge.

Together, these assumptions are sufficient to guarantee both uniform convergence of the functions and differentiability of the limit.

▶25:40

Importance of Interchanging Operations

This theorem is crucial because it allows us to safely interchange two infinite processes:

  • Taking limits (as $n \to \infty$)
  • Differentiation (which is itself a limiting process)

In general, we must be extremely careful when interchanging limiting operations. This theorem provides the precise conditions under which such interchange is valid.

▶26:15

Simpler Case: Continuous Derivatives

If we had the luxury of assuming that each $f_n'$ is continuous, the proof would be much more straightforward using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus:

$$f_n(x) = f_n(c) + \int_c^x f_n'(t) \, dt$$

However, the theorem makes no such assumption - it only requires differentiability, not continuity of the derivatives. This makes the proof more subtle and requires clever use of the Mean Value Theorem.

▶28:53

Proof Strategy

The proof has two main parts:

Part A: Show that $f_n \to f$ uniformly using the Mean Value Theorem and Cauchy criterion.

Part B: Show that $f$ is differentiable with $f' = g$ by interpreting differentiability as a continuity question.

Key insight for Part A: Apply the Mean Value Theorem to the difference $f_n - f_m$ rather than to individual functions. This clever trick allows us to use our assumption about uniform convergence of derivatives.

▶33:33

Proof of Part A: Uniform Convergence

By the Mean Value Theorem applied to $f_n - f_m$:

$$f_n(x) - f_m(x) = f_n(c) - f_m(c) + (x-c) \cdot (f_n' - f_m')(\xi_{n,m})$$

for some $\xi_{n,m}$ between $x$ and $c$.

Taking absolute values and using the triangle inequality:

$$|f_n(x) - f_m(x)| \leq |f_n(c) - f_m(c)| + |x-c| \cdot |(f_n' - f_m')(\xi_{n,m})|$$

$$\leq |f_n(c) - f_m(c)| + (b-a) \cdot \sup_{t \in [a,b]} |f_n'(t) - f_m'(t)|$$

Taking the supremum over $x$:

$$\sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f_n(x) - f_m(x)| \leq |f_n(c) - f_m(c)| + (b-a) \cdot \sup_{t \in [a,b]} |f_n'(t) - f_m'(t)|$$

Both terms on the right tend to 0 as $n,m \to \infty$ (first by Assumption 2, second by Assumption 1), so $(f_n)$ is uniformly Cauchy and hence uniformly convergent.

▶39:48

Proof of Part B: Differentiability

Clever idea: Instead of directly checking the difference quotient, we interpret differentiability as a continuity question.

For fixed $y$, define:

$$h(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{f(x) - f(y)}{x-y} & \text{if } x \neq y \\ g(y) & \text{if } x = y \end{cases}$$

Then $f$ is differentiable at $y$ with $f'(y) = g(y)$ if and only if $h$ is continuous at $y$.

Similarly, define $h_n(x)$ by replacing $f$ with $f_n$ and $g(y)$ with $f_n'(y)$.

Each $h_n$ is continuous (since $f_n$ is differentiable), and $h_n \to h$ pointwise. If we can show $h_n \to h$ uniformly, then $h$ is continuous by our previous theorem.

▶47:53

Completing the Proof

To show $h_n \to h$ uniformly, we use the Cauchy criterion again. For $x \neq y$:

$$h_n(x) - h_m(x) = \frac{(f_n - f_m)(x) - (f_n - f_m)(y)}{x-y}$$

By the Mean Value Theorem applied to $f_n - f_m$:

$$= (f_n' - f_m')(\xi)$$

for some $\xi$ between $x$ and $y$.

For $x = y$: $h_n(y) - h_m(y) = f_n'(y) - f_m'(y)$.

In both cases: $|h_n(x) - h_m(x)| \leq \sup_{t \in [a,b]} |f_n'(t) - f_m'(t)| \to 0$

Therefore $h_n \to h$ uniformly, so $h$ is continuous, so $f$ is differentiable with $f' = g$.

▶54:13

Conclusion

This theorem provides the precise conditions under which we can safely interchange differentiation and taking limits. The key requirements are:

  • Uniform convergence of the derivatives
  • Convergence of the sequence at at least one point

This completes our study of uniform convergence and its relationship with the fundamental operations of calculus: continuity, integration, and differentiation.
