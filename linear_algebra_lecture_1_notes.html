Course Overview

This course revisits familiar concepts from first year vectors and matrices, but with a very different approach. The emphasis shifts from concrete objects (column vectors, arrays of numbers) to the abstract algebraic perspective that pure mathematicians use.

We'll develop the language of abstract algebra to discuss these concepts, preparing for more advanced courses like representation theory and algebraic geometry. The course is also highly applicable, particularly for infinite dimensional vector spaces and function spaces.

Vector Space

An F-vector space (or vector space over F) consists of:

  1. An abelian group $(V, +)$ with underlying set $V$ and operation written as addition
  2. The identity element written as $\underline{0}$ (zero vector)
  3. A function $F \times V \to V$ called scalar multiplication, written as $(\lambda, v) \mapsto \lambda v$ or $\lambda \cdot v$

These operations satisfy the following axioms for all $v, w \in V$ and $\lambda, \mu \in F$:

  1. $(\lambda + \mu) \cdot v = \lambda \cdot v + \mu \cdot v$ (distributivity over field addition)
  2. $\lambda \cdot (v + w) = \lambda \cdot v + \lambda \cdot w$ (distributivity over vector addition)
  3. $(\lambda \mu) \cdot v = \lambda \cdot (\mu \cdot v)$ (associativity of scalar multiplication)
  4. $1 \cdot v = v$ (multiplicative identity acts as identity)
▶11:33

Notation and Terminology

Elements of V are called vectors and are not underlined (except the zero vector $\underline{0}$).

Elements of F are called scalars.

The zero vector $\underline{0}$ is the additive identity of the group $(V, +)$ and is distinct from the element $0$ in the field $F$.

▶12:47

Column Vectors

The most familiar example is $F^n$, the space of $n$-dimensional column vectors with entries in the field $F$:

  • $\mathbb{R}^n$ is the space of real $n$-dimensional column vectors
  • $\mathbb{C}^n$ is the space of complex $n$-dimensional column vectors

Addition is defined component-wise, and scalar multiplication is defined element-wise.

Important observation: $\mathbb{C}^n$ is both a complex vector space and a real vector space. We can restrict scalar multiplication to only real numbers, making it a real vector space as well.

▶15:33

Matrices

The space $M_{m,n}(F)$ of $m \times n$ matrices over the field $F$ forms a vector space. This is essentially the same as $F^{mn}$ (column vectors of length $mn$), just arranged in a rectangular format rather than a column.

Matrices have additional structure beyond their vector space structure (matrix multiplication, etc.).

▶17:15

Function Spaces

For any non-empty set $X$, the space $F^X$ of functions from $X$ to $F$ is a vector space with operations defined pointwise:

  • $(f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)$ for all $x \in X$
  • $(\lambda f)(x) = \lambda \cdot f(x)$ for all $x \in X$

This includes important subspaces like:

  • $C(\mathbb{R})$ - continuous real-valued functions
  • $C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ - infinitely differentiable functions
  • $P(\mathbb{R})$ - polynomial functions
▶19:17

Additional Properties

Other familiar properties of vector spaces follow from the axioms:

  • $0 \cdot v = \underline{0}$ (scalar multiplication by zero gives zero vector)
  • $(-1) \cdot v = -v$ (scalar multiplication by -1 gives additive inverse)

These can be verified as exercises using the given axioms.

Subspace

A subspace $U$ of an $F$-vector space $V$ is a subset $U \subseteq V$ that is itself an $F$-vector space under the same operations as $V$.

Equivalent condition: $U$ is a subspace if and only if:

  1. $(U, +)$ is a subgroup of $(V, +)$
  2. Scalar multiplication preserves $U$: for all $\lambda \in F$ and $u \in U$, we have $\lambda u \in U$
▶24:45

Subspace Test

A subset $U \subseteq V$ is a subspace if and only if:

  1. $U \neq \emptyset$ (non-empty)
  2. For all $u, w \in U$ and $\lambda \in F$: $u + \lambda w \in U$
▶26:27

Proof of Subspace Test

Forward direction: If $U$ is a subspace, then it's non-empty (contains $\underline{0}$) and closed under addition and scalar multiplication, so $u + \lambda w \in U$.

Reverse direction: Suppose $U$ satisfies both conditions. Taking $\lambda = -1$ in condition 2 gives $u - w \in U$ for all $u, w \in U$. Combined with non-emptiness, this shows $U$ is a subgroup by the subgroup test from group theory.

Taking $u = \underline{0}$ in condition 2 gives $\lambda w \in U$ for all $\lambda \in F$ and $w \in U$, so scalar multiplication preserves $U$. Therefore $U$ is a subspace.

▶31:22

Subspace Examples

Example 1: The set $\{(x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : x + y + z = t\}$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ if and only if $t = 0$.

Example 2: In the vector space of all functions $\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$:

  • $C(\mathbb{R})$ (continuous functions) is a subspace
  • $C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ (infinitely differentiable functions) is a subspace
  • $P(\mathbb{R})$ (polynomial functions) is a subspace

Each inclusion $P(\mathbb{R}) \subseteq C^\infty(\mathbb{R}) \subseteq C(\mathbb{R}) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{\mathbb{R}}$ is a subspace inclusion.

▶36:43

Intersection of Subspaces

If $U$ and $W$ are subspaces of $V$, then $U \cap W$ is also a subspace of $V$.

▶37:16

Proof of Intersection Property

Non-empty: Both $U$ and $W$ contain $\underline{0}$, so $\underline{0} \in U \cap W$.

Subspace test: For $x, y \in U \cap W$ and $\lambda \in F$:

  • Since $U$ is a subspace: $x + \lambda y \in U$
  • Since $W$ is a subspace: $x + \lambda y \in W$
  • Therefore: $x + \lambda y \in U \cap W$

Hence $U \cap W$ satisfies the subspace test.

▶39:57

Important Warning: Unions

The union of subspaces is almost never a subspace.

This is a common mistake. If you want a subspace that contains both $U$ and $W$, you should use the subspace sum instead.

▶41:22

Subspace Sum

For subspaces $U$ and $W$ of $V$, the subspace sum is defined as:

$$U + W = \{u + w : u \in U, w \in W\}$$

This is the smallest subspace of $V$ containing both $U$ and $W$.

▶42:09

Subspace Sum Properties

For any subspaces $U$ and $W$ of $V$:

  1. $U + W$ is always a subspace of $V$
  2. $U + W$ contains both $U$ and $W$
  3. $U + W$ is the smallest subspace containing both $U$ and $W$

Proof by subspace test (exercise).

▶45:22

Linear Map

A linear map (or linear transformation) from $V$ to $W$ is a function $\alpha: V \to W$ such that for all $u, v \in V$ and $\lambda \in F$:

  1. $\alpha(u + v) = \alpha(u) + \alpha(v)$ (preserves addition)
  2. $\alpha(\lambda v) = \lambda \alpha(v)$ (preserves scalar multiplication)

Equivalent condition: $\alpha(u + \lambda v) = \alpha(u) + \lambda \alpha(v)$ for all $u, v \in V$ and $\lambda \in F$.

▶48:02

Linear Map Examples

Matrix multiplication: For $A \in M_{m,n}(F)$, the map $\alpha: F^n \to F^m$ defined by $\alpha(v) = Av$ is linear.

Differentiation: The map $D: C^\infty(\mathbb{R}) \to C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ defined by $D(f) = f'$ is linear.

Integration: The map $I: C([0,1]) \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $I(f) = \int_0^1 f(x) \, dx$ is linear.

Evaluation: For $x_0 \in X$, the map $\text{ev}_{x_0}: F^X \to F$ defined by $\text{ev}_{x_0}(f) = f(x_0)$ is linear.

Identity and zero maps: $\text{id}_V: V \to V$ and $0: V \to W$ are always linear.

Composition: If $\alpha: V \to W$ and $\beta: U \to V$ are linear, then $\alpha \circ \beta: U \to W$ is linear.

▶52:36

Non-Linear Examples

Squaring: The map $\alpha: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $\alpha(x) = x^2$ is not linear.

Adding constant: The map $\beta: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $\beta(x) = x + 1$ is not linear.
