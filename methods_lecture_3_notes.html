Section 1.4: Periodic Extensions

We move on to section 1.4 about periodic extensions, building on the basics of Fourier series and convergence that we've established. We've shown that for functions that aren't too wild (with finitely many discontinuities and finitely many maxima and minima), the Fourier series converges to the function itself at points of continuity and to the average at points of discontinuity.

▶1:13

Even and Odd Extensions

Given a function $f: [0,L] \to \mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$), we can define two $2L$-periodic extensions:

Even extension: $f_{\text{even}}(\theta) = \begin{cases} f(\theta) & \text{if } \theta \in [0,L] \\ f(-\theta) & \text{if } \theta \in [-L,0] \end{cases}$

Odd extension: $f_{\text{odd}}(\theta) = \begin{cases} f(\theta) & \text{if } \theta \in [0,L] \\ -f(-\theta) & \text{if } \theta \in [-L,0] \end{cases}$

These extensions are then continued periodically with period $2L$.

▶4:51

Relationship to Original Function

Note that $f(\theta) = f_{\text{even}}(\theta) = f_{\text{odd}}(\theta)$ when we restrict to the original interval $[0,L]$. This is the key insight: we now have periodic functions that we can express as Fourier series, and these Fourier series will give us our original function when restricted to the interval $[0,L]$.

▶5:13

Cosine Series

For the even extension, since it's an even function, the Fourier series contains only cosine terms:

$$f_{\text{even}}(\theta) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \cos\left(\frac{n\pi\theta}{L}\right)$$

where the coefficients are:

$$a_n = \frac{2}{L} \int_0^L f(\theta) \cos\left(\frac{n\pi\theta}{L}\right) \, d\theta$$

This follows from the standard Fourier coefficient formula, using the fact that the even function and cosine are both even, allowing us to integrate only over $[0,L]$ and multiply by 2.

▶7:32

Sine Series

For the odd extension, the Fourier series contains only sine terms:

$$f_{\text{odd}}(\theta) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n \sin\left(\frac{n\pi\theta}{L}\right)$$

where the coefficients are:

$$b_n = \frac{2}{L} \int_0^L f(\theta) \sin\left(\frac{n\pi\theta}{L}\right) \, d\theta$$

This is analogous to the cosine series, using the same integration trick.

▶9:04

Cosine and Sine Series

For a function $f: [0,L] \to \mathbb{R}$, we define:

Cosine series: $\frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \cos\left(\frac{n\pi\theta}{L}\right)$

Sine series: $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n \sin\left(\frac{n\pi\theta}{L}\right)$

where $a_n$ and $b_n$ are defined as above. These series represent the even and odd periodic extensions of $f$, respectively.

▶10:30

Example: Constant Function

Consider $f(\theta) = 1$ on $[0,\pi]$.

Odd extension: This creates a square wave pattern: $+1$ on $(0,\pi)$, $-1$ on $(-\pi,0)$, etc.

Even extension: This simply extends the constant function: $+1$ everywhere.

For the sine series coefficients:

$$b_n = \frac{2}{\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \sin(n\theta) \, d\theta = \frac{2}{n\pi}(1 - (-1)^n)$$

This gives us the sine series representation of the constant function when restricted to $(0,\pi)$.

▶14:35

Example: Cosine Series for Constant Function

For the cosine series of $f(\theta) = 1$ on $[0,\pi]$:

We can verify that $a_0 = 2$ and $a_n = 0$ for $n \geq 1$.

This gives us the trivial cosine series: $1 = 1$ (since $\frac{a_0}{2} = 1$).

The even extension is continuous everywhere, so the Fourier series converges to the function everywhere on the interval.

▶16:15

Why Even and Odd Extensions?

The advantage of using even or odd extensions rather than a general periodic extension is that it reduces the computational work by half. With even extensions, we get only cosine terms; with odd extensions, only sine terms. A general periodic extension would require both sine and cosine terms, making the calculations more complex.

Section 1.5: Regularity and Decay of Fourier Coefficients

We now turn to understanding how the smoothness (regularity) of a function relates to how quickly its Fourier coefficients decay as $n \to \infty$.

▶17:06

Riemann-Lebesgue Lemma

This result is on the edge of being examinable but contains complex analysis: Non-examinable in detail.

Let $g: [a,b] \to \mathbb{C}$ be integrable and $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$. Then:

$$\int_a^b e^{-i\lambda\theta} g(\theta) \, d\theta \to 0 \quad \text{as } |\lambda| \to \infty$$

This is the famous Riemann-Lebesgue Lemma.

▶18:35

Fourier Coefficients Decay

If $f$ is an $L$-periodic integrable function, then the Fourier coefficients satisfy:

$$\hat{f}_n \to 0 \quad \text{as } |n| \to \infty$$

This follows from the Riemann-Lebesgue Lemma by taking $\lambda = \frac{2\pi n}{L}$ and noting that $\hat{f}_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_0^L f(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \, d\theta$.

▶21:35

Regularity and Decay Connection

There's a beautiful two-way relationship between function regularity and Fourier coefficient decay:

  • Forward direction: If you tell me how smooth your function is (how many continuous derivatives it has), I can predict how fast the Fourier coefficients decay.
  • Converse direction: If you tell me how fast the Fourier coefficients go to zero, I can determine how smooth the function is.

This is why we use harmonic analysis in PDEs - it's much easier to analyze decay rates than to zoom in and take limits to check smoothness.

▶23:26

Riemann-Lebesgue Lemma: Intuitive Explanation

Non-examinable detailed explanation

The integral $\int e^{-i\lambda\theta} g(\theta) \, d\theta$ represents the integral of $g(\theta)$ multiplied by rapidly oscillating terms $\cos(\lambda\theta)$ and $\sin(\lambda\theta)$.

As $\lambda \to \infty$, the oscillations become very rapid. The positive and negative contributions cancel out due to the rapid oscillation, leading to the integral tending to zero. This is a cancellation argument - high-frequency oscillations average out to zero when integrated against a nice function.

▶26:27

Decay Rate for Smooth Functions

Suppose $f$ is $L$-periodic and $f \in C^k(\mathbb{R})$ (meaning $f, f', f'', \ldots, f^{(k)}$ are all continuous on $\mathbb{R}$).

Then the Fourier coefficients satisfy:

$$|\hat{f}_n| = O\left(\frac{1}{|n|^k}\right) \quad \text{as } |n| \to \infty$$

That is, the smoother the function, the faster the Fourier coefficients decay.

▶27:41

Proof of Decay Rate

We use integration by parts repeatedly. Starting with:

$$\hat{f}_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_0^L f(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \, d\theta$$

Integrate by parts: integrate the exponential, differentiate $f$:

$$\hat{f}_n = \frac{L}{2\pi i n} \left[ f(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \right]_0^L + \frac{L}{2\pi i n} \int_0^L f'(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \, d\theta$$

Due to periodicity and continuity: $f(L) = f(0)$, so the boundary term vanishes. We get:

$$\hat{f}_n = \frac{L}{2\pi i n} \hat{f'}_n$$

Continuing this process $k$ times:

$$\hat{f}_n = \left(\frac{L}{2\pi i n}\right)^k \hat{f^{(k)}}_n$$

Since $f^{(k)}$ is continuous (hence integrable), $\hat{f^{(k)}}_n = O(1)$ by the Riemann-Lebesgue Lemma. Therefore $|\hat{f}_n| = O(1/|n|^k)$.

Section 1.6: Differentiation of Fourier Series

We now consider: if $f$ has Fourier series, what is the Fourier series of its derivative $f'$?

▶35:39

Differentiation of Fourier Series

Suppose $f$ is $L$-periodic and continuously differentiable on $\mathbb{R}$ with $f' = g$.

Then we can differentiate the Fourier series term by term:

$$\frac{d}{d\theta}\left(\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n e^{2\pi i n \theta / L}\right) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \frac{2\pi i n}{L} \hat{f}_n e^{2\pi i n \theta / L}$$

Key condition: This works only if $f$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ (not just on one period).

▶37:00

Proof of Differentiation Rule

The Fourier coefficient of the derivative is:

$$\hat{g}_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_0^L g(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \, d\theta = \frac{1}{L} \int_0^L f'(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \, d\theta$$

Using integration by parts (integrate the exponential, differentiate $f$):

$$\hat{g}_n = \frac{1}{L} \left[ f(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \right]_0^L + \frac{2\pi i n}{L^2} \int_0^L f(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \, d\theta$$

The boundary term becomes $f(L) - f(0)$. If $f$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$, then by periodicity $f(0) = f(L)$, so the boundary term vanishes and we get:

$$\hat{g}_n = \frac{2\pi i n}{L} \hat{f}_n$$

This shows that $\hat{f'}_n = \frac{2\pi i n}{L} \hat{f}_n$, which is exactly what we get by differentiating the Fourier series term by term.

Section 1.7: Parseval's Theorem

A very short section on Parseval's theorem, which relates the inner product of two functions to their Fourier coefficients.

▶42:32

Parseval's Theorem

For two $L$-periodic functions $f$ and $g$ with Fourier series, we have:

$$\langle f, g \rangle = L \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n \overline{\hat{g}_n}$$

where $\langle f, g \rangle = \int_0^L f(\theta) \overline{g(\theta)} \, d\theta$ is the inner product.

In the special case when $f = g$, this becomes:

$$\int_0^L |f(\theta)|^2 \, d\theta = L \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} |\hat{f}_n|^2$$

▶46:13

Example: Computing $\zeta(2)$ using Parseval's Theorem

Consider the $2\pi$-periodic function $f(\theta) = \theta$ on $[-\pi, \pi]$. This function is not continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ (it has jump discontinuities at $(2k+1)\pi$).

It can be shown (left as exercise) that the Fourier series is:

$$f(\theta) \sim \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{i(-1)^n}{n} e^{in\theta}$$

By Parseval's theorem:

$$\frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \theta^2 \, d\theta = \sum_{n \neq 0} \left|\frac{i(-1)^n}{n}\right|^2 = 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$$

Computing the left side: $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \theta^2 \, d\theta = 2\int_0^{\pi} \theta^2 \, d\theta = 2\frac{\pi^3}{3} = \frac{2\pi^3}{3}$, so:

$$\frac{1}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{2\pi^3}{3} = \frac{\pi^2}{3} = 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$$

Therefore: $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6} = \zeta(2)$.
