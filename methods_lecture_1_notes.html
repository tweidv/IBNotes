Section 1.1: Motivation

Around 1807, Joseph Fourier was studying heat conduction along a metal rod. For a cylindrical rod, measuring the temperature on top of the rod, if you go around in radians by another $2\pi$, you're at the same point on the rod. Therefore, the temperature should be a $2\pi$-periodic function.

▶02:47

$2\pi$-Periodic Functions

A function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is called $2\pi$-periodic if:

$$f(\theta + 2\pi) = f(\theta) \quad \text{for all } \theta \in \mathbb{R}$$

▶03:43

Fourier's Discovery

Fourier discovered that if $f(\theta)$ is a $2\pi$-periodic function, it can be written in the form:

$$f(\theta) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n e^{in\theta}$$

where the sum is over all integers $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

▶04:25

Fourier Coefficients

The coefficients $\hat{f}_n$ in the Fourier series are given by the formula:

$$\hat{f}_n = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta) e^{-in\theta} \, d\theta$$

where $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ (any integer).

▶05:07

Fourier's Grand Claim

Fourier believed this worked for any $2\pi$-periodic function. That is:

  1. Given any $2\pi$-periodic function $f$
  2. Compute each $\hat{f}_n$ using the formula above
  3. Construct the sum $\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n e^{in\theta}$
  4. This sum would return your original function!

Note: This claim is actually not quite correct in its original form - it requires certain conditions on the function.

▶07:49

Historical Context

This was before the birth of analysis as we know it today. Fourier made the claim without attempting to prove it rigorously - back in those days, you just asserted mathematical statements. However, he put it to excellent use in developing a wonderful theory of heat conduction.

Lesson: Learn to be brave with mathematics, like physicists are. They manipulate infinite sums, eliminate inconvenient infinities, and develop quantum field theories that agree with experiment - all by being brave and not worrying about complete justification.

▶08:48

Section 1.2: Modern Treatment

Let's make this slightly more mathematical with a modern approach.

▶09:13

Vector Space of $L$-Periodic Functions

Introduce a vector space $V$ of $L$-periodic functions:

$$V = \{f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C} \mid f \text{ is "nice" and } f(\theta + L) = f(\theta) \text{ for all } \theta \in \mathbb{R}\}$$

Here we allow complex-valued functions. The term "nice" is a technical condition (to be defined more precisely later) - the nicer the function, the more we can do with it.

We assume the property of niceness is preserved under addition and scalar multiplication, so $V$ is indeed a vector space.

▶10:58

Note on Periodic Functions

For $f \in V$, we need only consider values of $f$ taken in an interval of length $L$, e.g., $[0, L]$ or $[-L/2, L/2]$.

Reason: Once we know what the function does in one period, periodicity tells us what it does everywhere else.

A generic real-valued function in this vector space might have jump discontinuities but still be reasonably nice on these intervals.

▶13:00

Inner Product on $V$

We introduce an inner product on $V$ defined by:

$$\langle f, g \rangle = \int_0^L f(\theta) \overline{g(\theta)} \, d\theta$$

where $f, g \in V$, and the bar denotes complex conjugation.

This satisfies all the required properties of an inner product.

▶14:00

Associated Norm

The associated norm is defined as:

$$\|f\| = \sqrt{\langle f, f \rangle} = \sqrt{\int_0^L |f(\theta)|^2 \, d\theta}$$

This gives us a nice inner product space structure.

▶14:29

The Functions $e_n$

For $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, consider the functions $e_n$ defined by:

$$e_n(\theta) = e^{2\pi i n \theta / L}$$

These are extremely nice functions - they are smooth, in fact analytic (coinciding with their Taylor series which converges locally uniformly everywhere). They are clearly $L$-periodic, so $e_n \in V$ for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

▶15:34

Orthogonality of the $e_n$ Functions

The inner product of two basis functions is:

$$\langle e_n, e_m \rangle = \int_0^L e^{2\pi i (n-m) \theta / L} \, d\theta = L \cdot \delta_{nm}$$

where $\delta_{nm}$ is the Kronecker delta (1 if $n = m$, 0 otherwise).

Consequences:

  • The $e_n$ are orthogonal in our vector space
  • $\|e_n\|^2 = L$ for all $n$
▶17:52

Analogy with 1A Vectors and Matrices

This looks like 1A Vectors and Matrices! Let's recall some concepts from that finite-dimensional setting to guide our intuition.

Recall: If $V_n$ is an $n$-dimensional vector space equipped with the usual inner product (dot product), and if $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ are orthogonal vectors each with length $\sqrt{L}$, then we can use such a set of vectors as a basis.

For each $x \in V_n$, we can write:

$$x = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \hat{x}_n e_n$$

for some complex numbers $\hat{x}_n$.

▶20:21

Finding Coefficients in Finite Dimensions

To find $\hat{x}_n$, we dot both sides with $e_m$:

$$x \cdot e_m = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \hat{x}_n (e_n \cdot e_m) = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \hat{x}_n L \delta_{nm} = L\hat{x}_m$$

Therefore:

$$\hat{x}_n = \frac{1}{L} x \cdot e_n$$

We found the coefficients in terms of the original vector $x$ and the orthogonal vectors $e_n$.

▶22:35

Could This Work on $V$?

Our vector space $V$ is not finite-dimensional. For example, every finite subset of the countable collection $\{e_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$ is linearly independent.

We're now entering the realm of 18th century mathematics (vs. 20th and 21st century rigor). We're not going to worry about full justification - we'll be brave!

▶23:54

Key Assumption

Let's assume that for any $f \in V$, we can write:

$$f(\theta) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n e_n(\theta) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n e^{2\pi i n \theta / L}$$

Given this assumption, what can we derive?

▶24:45

Deriving the Formula for Fourier Coefficients

Just as in the finite-dimensional case, we take the inner product of both sides with $e_m$:

$$\langle f, e_m \rangle = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n \langle e_n, e_m \rangle = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n L \delta_{nm} = L\hat{f}_m$$

Therefore:

$$\hat{f}_n = \frac{1}{L} \langle f, e_n \rangle = \frac{1}{L} \int_0^L f(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \, d\theta$$

Important note: This derivation involves interchanging a sum and an integral (both are limiting processes). This would require justification - typically uniform convergence - which we're not providing. When functions are sufficiently nice, this can be justified.

This is precisely Fourier's formula (with $L = 2\pi$ in the original case)!

▶27:49

Complex Fourier Series

For an $L$-periodic function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C}$, we define its complex Fourier series by:

$$\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n e^{2\pi i n \theta / L}$$

where the complex Fourier coefficients are:

$$\hat{f}_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_0^L f(\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L} \, d\theta$$

▶29:56

Notation

For $f \in V$, we write:

$$f(\theta) \sim \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n e^{2\pi i n \theta / L}$$

The symbol $\sim$ means: "the series on the right-hand side corresponds to the complex Fourier series for the function on the left-hand side."

Note: We would like to replace $\sim$ with $=$ (equality), but this needs justification, which we'll address in the next lecture.

▶32:21

Real Fourier Series (Sines and Cosines)

You may have seen Fourier series in terms of sines and cosines rather than exponentials. Fourier's original work used trigonometric sines and cosines. The presentation is slightly messier but sometimes useful.

Let's show how to go from the exponential form to the sine/cosine form.

▶32:49

Real Fourier Series

We can split the complex Fourier series into parts where $n = 0$, $n > 0$, and $n < 0$:

$$\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}_n e^{2\pi i n \theta / L} = \hat{f}_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \hat{f}_n e^{2\pi i n \theta / L} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \hat{f}_{-n} e^{-2\pi i n \theta / L}$$

Using Euler's formula $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$, this becomes:

$$\hat{f}_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[(\hat{f}_n + \hat{f}_{-n})\cos\frac{2\pi n\theta}{L} + i(\hat{f}_n - \hat{f}_{-n})\sin\frac{2\pi n\theta}{L}\right]$$

Defining for $n \geq 0$:

  • $a_n = \hat{f}_n + \hat{f}_{-n}$
  • $b_n = i(\hat{f}_n - \hat{f}_{-n})$

The (real) Fourier series is:

$$\frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[a_n \cos\frac{2\pi n\theta}{L} + b_n \sin\frac{2\pi n\theta}{L}\right]$$

where the Fourier coefficients are:

$$a_n = \frac{2}{L} \int_0^L f(\theta) \cos\frac{2\pi n\theta}{L} \, d\theta$$

$$b_n = \frac{2}{L} \int_0^L f(\theta) \sin\frac{2\pi n\theta}{L} \, d\theta$$

▶39:28

Even and Odd Functions

The real Fourier series representation is particularly useful for even or odd functions:

  • If $f$ is even, then all $b_n = 0$ (only cosine terms remain)
  • If $f$ is odd, then all $a_n = 0$ (only sine terms remain)
▶40:55

Orthogonality of Sines and Cosines

Define $c_n(\theta) = \cos\frac{2\pi n\theta}{L}$ and $s_n(\theta) = \sin\frac{2\pi n\theta}{L}$.

Then for $n, m \geq 1$:

  • $\langle c_n, c_m \rangle = \langle s_n, s_m \rangle = \frac{L}{2} \delta_{nm}$
  • $\langle c_n, 1 \rangle = \langle s_m, 1 \rangle = \langle c_n, s_m \rangle = 0$

Therefore, the system $\{1, c_1, c_2, \ldots, s_1, s_2, \ldots\}$ is also an orthogonal set in $V$.

Proof left as an exercise (Example Sheet 1, Question 1).

▶43:09

Technical Note on Symmetric Limits

One technical point: for the complex Fourier series $\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$, we have a sum with two "tails" (positive and negative integers). This is clearly a limit.

For the real Fourier series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}$, it's clear how to define the limit (partial sums up to $N$).

Important: For equivalent statements in the exponential setting, you must take the symmetric limit: $\sum_{n=-N}^{N}$, not separate limits for positive and negative terms.

▶43:16

Computing a Fourier Series

Let $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued, period-1 function such that:

$$f(\theta) = \theta(1 - \theta) \quad \text{for } \theta \in [0, 1]$$

Always draw the graph before computing the Fourier series! The graph tells you about regularity (continuity, differentiability) and this tells you about the decay rate of Fourier coefficients.

The graph shows this is an even function that's continuous everywhere but not quite differentiable at integer points (corners). This is a reasonably nice function.

▶45:55

Computing Complex Fourier Coefficients

For $n \neq 0$:

$$\hat{f}_n = \frac{1}{1} \int_0^1 \theta(1-\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta} \, d\theta$$

Key technique: Integrate by parts! Integrate the exponential and differentiate the other part.

First integration by parts:

$$= \left[-\frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{2\pi i n} e^{-2\pi i n \theta}\right]_0^1 + \frac{1}{2\pi i n} \int_0^1 (1-2\theta) e^{-2\pi i n \theta} \, d\theta$$

The boundary term vanishes (at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = 1$, the function $\theta(1-\theta) = 0$).

Second integration by parts:

$$= \frac{1}{2\pi i n} \left[\left(-\frac{(1-2\theta)}{2\pi i n} e^{-2\pi i n \theta}\right)_0^1 + \frac{1}{2\pi i n} \int_0^1 2 e^{-2\pi i n \theta} \, d\theta\right]$$

The boundary term gives: $\frac{1}{2\pi i n} \left[-\frac{1}{2\pi i n} - \frac{1}{2\pi i n}\right] = -\frac{1}{(2\pi i n)^2}$

For the integral term: if $n \neq 0$, $\int_0^1 e^{-2\pi i n \theta} \, d\theta = 0$.

Therefore:

$$\hat{f}_n = -\frac{1}{2\pi^2 n^2} \quad \text{for } n \neq 0$$

For $n = 0$:

$$\hat{f}_0 = \int_0^1 \theta(1-\theta) \, d\theta = \left[\frac{\theta^2}{2} - \frac{\theta^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6}$$

▶48:46

The Fourier Series Result

Therefore, the complex Fourier series is:

$$f(\theta) \sim \frac{1}{6} - \frac{1}{2\pi^2} \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{e^{2\pi i n \theta}}{n^2}$$

In terms of sines and cosines (using the even function property):

$$f(\theta) \sim \frac{1}{6} - \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\cos(2\pi n\theta)}{\pi^2 n^2}$$

Note: only cosine terms appear, as expected for an even function!

▶49:50

Verification and Convergence

We'd really like this to be an equality (not just $\sim$). Let's check at $\theta = 0$:

  • Left side: $f(0) = 0$
  • Right side: $\frac{1}{6} - \frac{1}{\pi^2}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$

This looks promising! You can also check at $\theta = 1/2$ and find it works.

By the Weierstrass M-test (from Analysis II), because of the rate of convergence ($1/n^2$), we know the right-hand side is a continuous function. The right-hand side is even, and we've checked it equals $f$ at several points.

It's beginning to look like Fourier's claim was correct - at least for sufficiently nice functions!
